\section{Introduction} \label{sec:intro}

With the rapid growth of Internet-of-Things (IoT) deployment for several daily life applications, the security of these devices is a key concern. Especially when the existing cryptography-based security techniques are resource-hungry and still vulnerable. Therefore, innovative and robust security mechanisms are urgently needed to protect the IoT end devices from malicious actors. 

Radio Frequency Fingerprint Identification (RFF) is an emerging technology and has been seen as a key candidate for device authentication at the physical layer \cite{AqeelSurvey}. The RF fingerprints are extracted from the received radio signal impaired by the transmitter's hardware components. These impairments are subtle, though unique to each transmitter, making them useful for device identification. However, manual extraction of these features is challenging and time-consuming. Therefore, Deep Learning (DL) based RFFI has been proposed as a vital solution for this field. Although DL based RFFI has shown enormous potential for emitter identification, but these algorithms require a massive amount of training. Availability of quality radio signal data is a key challenge in this domain \cite{AqeelSurvey}.

This report presents our efforts to collect Long-range (LoRa) RF signal data during the one-month BIR-supported summer internship period from July to August 2025. We systematically established test-bed scenarios in the indoor and outdoor environments to collect diverse LoRa RF signal data.

\subsection{Chirp Spread Spectrum modulation}\label{CSS}

LoRa is a long-range, low-power, proprietary radio interface.
It is based on a Chirp Spread Spectrum (CSS) modulation technique. 
The basic element of this technique is called a chirp. 
A chirp is a sinusoidal signal whose frequency varies linearly over time. 
Details on the underlying math can be found in a recent article \cite{CSSTuto} explaining CSS modulation and the key advances made to understand it.
To understand the experiments made in this paper, only the frequency domain is explored and explained. The Figure \ref{fig:timeVsFreq} represents briefly the relation between the frequency and time domain. 

\begin{figure}[ht]
  \begin{center}
    \includesvg[width=0.70\textwidth]{images/timeVsFreq.svg}
  \end{center}
  \caption{Representation of a LoRa symbol in the time domain (Top) and the frequency domain (bottom).}\label{fig:timeVsFreq}
\end{figure}

There are three necessary parameters for LoRa communication : a carrier frequency ($F_c$), a bandwidth ($B$) and spreading factor ($SF$).\\
The carrier frequency of the signal is set in Europe as \qty{868}{\mega\hertz}.
Finally, the spreading factor represents the number of bits carried in a LoRa symbol. For example, with an SF of 7, a LoRa symbol can represent a value ranging from 0 to $2^7 -1 = 127$.

LoRa symbols are chrips whose duration $T$ depends on the bandwidth $B$ and spreading factor $SF$ as the equation \ref{eq:symbolDuration} shows. 
\begin{equation}
  T = \frac{2^{SF}}{B} 
  \label{eq:symbolDuration}
\end{equation}
A LoRa symbol frequency varies from $F_c - B/2$ to $F_c + B/2$ linearly as shown in the Figure \ref{chirps.symb}. It implies that when a signal reaches a frequency of $F_c + B/2$ (resp. $F_c -B/2$), wraps up to $F_c - B/2$ (resp. $F_c + B/2$) before varying linearly again.

The value represented depends on the start (and final) frequency of the LoRa symbol.
For a symbol of value s, the starting frequency $f_0 = F_c - B/2 + \frac{sB}{2^{SF}}$.
Intuitively, the value is represented by the "frequency phase" of the received signal.  
It is possible to observe that the symbol represented in Figure \ref{chirps.base-upchirp} is a baseup chirp because its starting frequency is $F_c - B/2$.\\
A baseup chirp represents a value of 0 and varies from $F_c - B/2$ to $F_c + B/2$. Similarly, a base-down chirp starts at $F_c + B/2$ and ends at $F_c - B/2$, decreasing linearly rather than increasing.

\subsection{Preamble}\label{preamble}
A LoRa frame is composed of 3 parts: the preamble part, the Start of Frame Delimiter (SFD) and the actual data symbols.
The preamble part is composed of 8 or more baseup chirps and a sync word part composed of two symbols.
The SFD is composed of 2.25 base-down chirps. 

A captured representation of a preamble + SFD can be seen in Figure \ref{fig:wholePream}.

\begin{figure}[ht]
  \begin{center}
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{images/LoRaPreambleWithText.png}
  \end{center}
  \caption[test 12]{Spectrogram of a captured LoRa preamble and SFD from inspectrum\footnotemark}\label{fig:wholePream}

The preamble consists of unmodulated, identical base-up chirps generated directly from the transmitter’s RF chain without data encoding. This makes it ideal for capturing hardware-specific distortions such as carrier frequency offset (CFO), I/Q imbalance, and phase noise—core features used in RF fingerprinting. The SFD is shorter and may not reveal stable and consistent features. Whereas the payload can vary in size, hence it cannot be considered suitable for DL based RFFI \cite{shen2021radio, al2021deeplora}.

\subsection{Demodulation}\label{demod}
The demodulation technique used in the experiments comes from a comprehensive paper named \textit{From Demodulation to Decoding} \cite{demToDec}. The method proposed can be described in 4 steps graphically explained in Figure \ref{fig:demToD}.

i. Retrieve the sampled data containing a LoRa symbol with a sampling frequency $f_{samp}$ of at least two times the bandwidth $Bw$.

ii. Multiply the retrieved signal, of initial frequency $f_0$, with a base downchirp to obtain two constant signals with frequency $f_0$ and $f_0 -Bw$.

iii. Apply a Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) on the result of the multiplication. This gives two distinct peaks at $f_0$ and $f_{samp} - Bw + f_0$ as shown in Figure \ref{fig:demToD}(iii).

iv. The paper then proposes two options to merge those peaks. A Fine-Grained Phase Alignment (FPA) and a Coarse-grained Phase Alignment. CPA is much less computationally heavy than FPA and has marginally worst results.
In either case, the result gives an FFT peak at $f_0$.

\subsection{Transmission method}\label{tranMeth}

The method to transmit and receive frames is discussed with two proposed methods. In each of them, the devices take turns one after the others. The difference is how each device handles its turn.

\textbf{Round-robin method (RR)} : on its turn, a device transmits a LoRa frame, then passes the turn to the next one. This is done in a cycling manner, meaning the last device gives the turn to the first one.
The method's transmissions goes on until every frame from every device is sent.

\textbf{One by One method (ObO)} : on its turn, a device transmits every LoRa frames it has to, then passes the turn to the next one. Hence, no cycles are needed because at the end of the last device's turn, the transmissions are finished. 

\textbf{Comparison} : the methods are compared by (1) speed, (2) ease of implementation, (3) misclassification probability and (4) quality. A misclassification occurs when the receiver labels a frame as coming from a device, although in reality, it's coming from another device. Misclassifications cannot occur, otherwise the database is unusable without finding and removing them. The quality metric refers to the uniformity of the captured frames.  

(1) Each method is similar in terms of speed, though ObO can be faster because there is no increase in the possible misclassifications as speed increases. 

(2) Both implementations have their specificities, but none are substantially harder to implement than the other.

(3) Misclassifications are most likely to occur when changing devices. The number of changes in RR makes it prone to misclassifications. In contrast, ObO has way fewer device changes, making it less prone to misclassification.

(4) The environment can vary over time (temperature, humidity, \dots) and the Deep Learning model is capable of identifying those variations and classify devices with respect to them.\\
In RR, those variations are present on all devices uniformly because they take turns quickly. \\
On the other hand, in ObO, those variations aren't present uniformly across all devices because the time between each device is much longer.
For example, between the transmissions of the first and last device, all the other devices sent their frames, which takes a considerable amount of time. This elapsed time can imply different variations between the first and last device, making the deep learning model capable of more easily distinguishing between them.

\textbf{Selection} : although the quality of the captures from ObO can be lower than in RR, because of the lack of time and difficulty to make the Round-robin method work flawlessly, the One by One method is used in the experiments.

\section{Implementation}\label{Impl}
The implementation stretches over 3 devices : the receiver laptop, the transmitter laptop and the Arduino devices. The objective of the implementation is to synchronize the transmission and reception such that the receiver is sure of the origin of each frame to correctly label it. 

The overall interactions of each device are presented. For more details the implementation is available on GitHub at \url{https://github.com/Gnarks/BIR}. All the codes are upgraded versions of Maxime Bazin's 2024 implementation \cite{kazawai}. \unsure{footnote or citation ?}

The implementation has 3 main processes : initiation (Section \ref{impl:initiation}), transmission (Section \ref{impl:transmission}) and termination (Section \ref{impl:termination}).
In addition, error handling for unreachable Arduino devices is discussed in Section \ref{impl:errorHandling}.


\subsection{Initiation}\label{impl:initiation}
The initiation process has 2 phases. First, the transmitter establishes communication with each Arduino device through USB and initiates them. Then, Once each device has acknowledged, the transmitter initiates the communication with the receiver, giving it the ordered list of devices, an initial time stamp to start the transmission process, and the period between two transmissions. For the second phase to be possible the receiver has to be in a waiting state before the initial communication.

\subsection{Transmission}\label{impl:transmission}
The transmission process is the main part of the implementation, it is in charge of the synchronization and labeling of the devices.
After the initiation, each laptop starts at the shared time. Then each period, the transmitter laptop makes a device send a frame and the receiver labels it with respect of the number of periods elapsed since the starting time.
Once every frame from a device are sent, the transmitter tells the receiver which device is sending its frames next with another start time.   
Such that, both the transmitter and receiver, at any time, know which device is currently transmitting and which frame is transmitted. \\

\subsection{Termination}\label{impl:termination}
The termination process is the last step of the implementation.
It occurs when every device sent every frame it had to. The transmitter waits a certain time before telling the receiver to stop the reception process and closes the communication.

\subsection{Error Handling}\label{impl:errorHandling}
If for some reason during initiation or transmission, an Arduino device is unreachable it is removed it from list of devices. It is mandatory to remove the device from the list to avoid it to take time slots to send without transmitting anything. 

An unreachable device can occur due to hardware or software issues, for example it is possible for a device to lose USB connection due the wind causing a false contact. Software issues are less likely to occur but taken into account. 

During initiation, if any device is unreachable, an error blocks the process. This prevents any false start and requires fixing the issue before restarting.

During transmission, if any device is unreachable, the transmitter stops then tells the receiver to remove this device from the list before continuing back where it needs after a shared time.

The process of removing a device from the transmission and reception list is irreversible, to avoid backtracking frames. It can be an issue if errors occur frequently, but in the case of our experiments, those errors rarely occurred. In addition, most of these errors occurred during the initiation step because of false contacts.

\section{Conclusion}\label{conclu}
As a conclusion, our efforts on collecting LoRa RF signal data during the internship as concluded in two datasets varying distance indoor and outdoor.
Although the time of the internship was small, a lot of improvements are to be mentioned from last year experiments.
Such as a more stable hardware structure without power issues and new synchronization methods.


\subsection{Possible improvements}
The possible improvements to be made in regard to this internship are the following :

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Make the RR method work stably for better quality captures. 
  \item Not relying on a mobile phone modem for communication between laptops.
  \item Fine-tune the detection algorithm to make it faster and safer.
\end{enumerate}
1) For example it might be possible to use a single laptop as the receiver and transmitter and use USB extension to connect to the SDR with longer distances. \\
2) A Ethernet cable might be more appropriate and faster.\\
3) Fine-tuning the different parameters such as the 0-padding or change the sampling frequency to $f-s = 2*BW$ as suggested a recent article \cite{CSSTuto}.
